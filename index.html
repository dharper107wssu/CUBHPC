Audiences We Center

Built for clinicians the field has historically treated as an afterthought

We lead with the communities most present in this work and least served by existing CE offerings. This is not a niche — this is the majority of the behavioral health workforce.

Primary Audience Track 1

Black Cisgender Women Clinicians, 50+

The unacknowledged senior workforce. Twenty-plus years of holding institutions together, often without title, recognition, or succession planning. These clinicians deserve training that sees their full complexity: career transitions, mentoring without burning out, legacy-building, and the specific neuroscience of chronic workplace stress in racialized environments. We build a track specifically for them — not a sidebar, a centerpiece.

Primary Audience Track 2

Black Queer Cisgender Women Clinicians, 35+

Navigating intersecting identities in clinical spaces, supervisory relationships, and organizational culture simultaneously. These clinicians often hold the most complex identities in any room — and the fewest CE trainings that speak to their actual experience. Our training explicitly names how queer identity, Blackness, and professional authority interact in clinical work, supervision, and institutional belonging.

Community Ethics Focus

BIPOC Clinicians & Community Ethics

Community ethics names what standard APA ethics codes don't: the clinician who lives in the same neighborhood as their clients, the supervisor whose supervisee is also a colleague at their house of worship, the cultural broker who is expected to translate trauma and navigate dual roles simultaneously. We dedicate a full ethics CE module to what community embeddedness actually means — and how to practice with integrity inside it.

"The field asked BIPOC clinicians to heal the wounds of structural racism while offering them no training for what that actually costs — and no ethics framework for what it actually requires."

Competitive Differentiation

What makes BHP Collective training irreplaceable

There are hundreds of CE training providers in DC and Maryland. Here is the precise and honest case for why ours belong in a category of their own.

Facilitator credential

The only IO + Clinical PhD pair in the region

A PhD in Industrial-Organizational Psychology and a PhD in Clinical Psychology in the same room is genuinely rare. The IO lens transforms individual clinical training into organizational analysis — and opens conversations no clinical-only trainer can have.

Neuroscience integration

Real brain science, not metaphor

Our neuroscience-trained clinician brings peer-reviewed science into every applicable training. Implicit bias, allostatic load, burnout, and performance psychology are all grounded in actual neuroscience literature — which makes the content stick and satisfy continuing education standards simultaneously.

Sports psychology application

Clinicians as high-performance practitioners

No other behavioral health CE program in this region uses sports psychology frameworks to teach clinical performance, supervision coaching, and burnout recovery. This is a genuine intellectual differentiator that attracts a new audience and creates a memorable brand position.

Population specificity

Trainings designed for the workforce doing the work

Black women clinicians over 50. Black queer clinicians navigating intersecting identities. BIPOC clinicians in community-embedded practice. Multilingual clinicians navigating supervision ethics. We build for these populations specifically — not as a diversity add-on, but as the primary frame.

Community ethics gap

The only community ethics CE in the region

No existing ethics CE training addresses the realities of BIPOC clinicians who practice inside the communities where they live. This is a documented gap, a real ethical risk, and an enormous unmet need in both MD and DC markets.

Multilingual supervision

A field-wide blind spot. Our specialty.

Maryland and DC have among the most linguistically diverse behavioral health workforces in the country. The ethics and practice of supervising multilingual clinicians are nearly entirely unaddressed in existing CE literature. BHP Collective owns this space.

Full Workshop Outlines

The three core workshops, fully built out

Learning objectives, module breakdowns, and key focus areas for the three primary trainings — ready for CE board submission and facilitator preparation.

Workshop 1 • 3 CE Hours • Structural Racism

The Water We Swim In: Structural Racism as a Clinical Reality

3 instructional hours • All licensure types • MD and DC approved • Qualifies for diversity CE hours

Learning Objectives — Participants will be able to:

  1. Identify at least three specific historical policies that produced measurable disparities in behavioral health outcomes for Black and Brown communities
  2. Explain the relationship between allostatic load and structural racism using current neuroscience evidence
  3. Apply positionality mapping to their own clinical practice to identify blind spots and structural assumptions
  4. Develop one structural action item specific to their clinical or supervisory role

Module Breakdown

Module 1 (45 min)
  • The architecture of structural racism
  • Redlining, its ongoing effects on access to care
  • Deinstitutionalization and criminalization
  • Racialized diagnostic history
Module 2 (45 min)
  • Allostatic load: the body under structural stress
  • Epigenetics and intergenerational trauma
  • Why "resilience" language can mask structural harm
  • Clinical implications for assessment and treatment
Module 3 (45 min)
  • IO lens: how organizations replicate structural racism
  • Behavioral health organizations as systems
  • Clinician positionality and power
  • Live positionality mapping exercise
Module 4 (45 min)
  • From awareness to action: what clinicians can actually do
  • Advocacy, documentation, and referral as structural interventions
  • Structural action planning tool
  • Q&A and integration
Workshop 2 • 3 CE Hours • Implicit Bias + Neuroscience

Wired and Hired: Implicit Bias From Neuroscience to the Therapy Room

3 instructional hours • All licensure types • MD and DC approved • Qualifies for diversity CE hours

Learning Objectives — Participants will be able to:

  1. Describe the neurological basis of implicit bias including dual process theory and the role of the amygdala and prefrontal cortex
  2. Identify at least three ways implicit bias manifests in clinical decision-making across the treatment continuum
  3. Apply at least two evidence-based debiasing strategies to their clinical or supervisory practice
  4. Conduct a structured bias audit on at least one case from their own caseload using the provided framework

Module Breakdown

Module 1 (45 min)
  • How the brain categorizes: dual process theory
  • Amygdala, threat response, and social categorization
  • Affect heuristic and clinical pattern-matching
  • In-group favoritism and out-group othering
Module 2 (45 min)
  • Bias in diagnosis: overdiagnosis and underdiagnosis by race
  • Bias in treatment planning: what research shows
  • Discharge and termination bias
  • IO lens: hiring and performance review bias
Module 3 (45 min)
  • Why awareness alone doesn't work (and the research that proves it)
  • Evidence-based debiasing protocols
  • Structural interventions vs. individual interventions
  • Slowing down: implementation intentions
Module 4 (45 min)
  • Live bias audit exercise
  • Structured case review framework
  • Integration and action planning
  • Q&A and closing
Workshop 3 • 12 CE Hours • 2-Day Supervision Intensive

Supervisors Who Build Worlds: Liberation-Centered Clinical Supervision Intensive

12 instructional hours across 2 days • LCPC, LCSW-C, LPC, LICSW • MD BAS eligible • DC supervisor endorsement eligible

Learning Objectives — Participants will be able to:

  1. Identify and apply at least two models of clinical supervision (MCST, developmental, feminist) to their current or anticipated supervisory practice
  2. Analyze how power operates in the supervisory relationship and describe at least three strategies for ethical use of supervisory authority
  3. Design a supervision agreement that incorporates identity-conscious evaluation criteria and explicit feedback protocols
  4. Apply trauma-informed supervision principles to at least one case involving a supervisee with lived trauma experience
  5. Provide corrective feedback using a culturally humble framework that distinguishes between performance, fit, and identity
  6. Articulate the specific ethical obligations of supervisors under MD and DC licensing law

Day 1 — The Architecture of Supervision

Morning (3 hrs)
  • Models of supervision: MCST, developmental, feminist, liberation-centered
  • Identity-conscious evaluation: separating performance from fit from identity
  • The supervisory relationship as a microcosm of systemic dynamics
  • Introduction to the supervision agreement drafting lab
Afternoon (3 hrs)
  • Power, gatekeeping, and the ethics of supervision
  • MD and DC licensing law: what supervisors are legally responsible for
  • Informed consent in supervision
  • Supervision agreement drafting lab (live working session)

Day 2 — The Practice of Supervision

Morning (3 hrs)
  • Trauma-informed supervision: recognizing and responding to supervisee trauma
  • Parallel process: how client dynamics live in the supervision room
  • Vicarious trauma in supervisors: neuroscience and self-protection
  • Sports psychology coaching model applied to supervision
Afternoon (3 hrs)
  • Culturally humble corrective feedback
  • Live supervision simulation with structured debrief
  • Supervising multilingual clinicians: ethics and practice
  • Integration, Q&A, and certificate of completion
